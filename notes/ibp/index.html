Toy example: IBP for tadpole integrals

Let's look at the simplest toy example, the family of one-loop tadpole integrals with internal mass mmm,

Iν=∫dDkiπD/21(−k2+m2)ν=∫dDkiπD/21ρν ,\begin{aligned} I_{\nu} &= \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \frac{1} {(-k^2+m^2)^{\nu}} \\ &= \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \frac 1 {\rho^{\nu}} \, , \end{aligned}Iν​​=∫iπD/2dDk​(−k2+m2)ν1​=∫iπD/2dDk​ρν1​,​

where we defined

ρ=−k2+m2 . \rho = -k^2 + m^2 \, . ρ=−k2+m2.

The IBP identity is

0=∫dDkiπD/2∂∂kμkμρν=∫dDkiπD/2[Dρν−νρν+1kμ∂∂kμρ] .\begin{aligned} 0 &= \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial k^\mu} \frac{k^\mu}{\rho^{\nu} } &= \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \left[ \frac {D} {\rho^\nu} - \frac {\nu} {\rho^{\nu+1}} k^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial k^\mu} \rho \right] \, . \end{aligned}0​=∫iπD/2dDk​∂kμ∂​ρνkμ​​=∫iπD/2dDk​[ρνD​−ρν+1ν​kμ∂kμ∂​ρ].​

We use

kμ∂∂kμρ=−2k2=2(ρ−m2) . k^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial k^\mu} \rho = -2k^2 = 2 (\rho - m^2) \, . kμ∂kμ∂​ρ=−2k2=2(ρ−m2).

The IBP identity becomes

0=∫dDkiπD/2[D−2νρν+2m2νρν+1]=(D−2ν)Iν+2m2νIν+1 . 0 = \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \left[ \frac {D - 2 \nu} {\rho^\nu} + \frac{2 m^2 \nu}{\rho^{\nu+1}} \right] = (D-2\nu) I_\nu + 2m^2 \nu I_{\nu+1} \, . 0=∫iπD/2dDk​[ρνD−2ν​+ρν+12m2ν​]=(D−2ν)Iν​+2m2νIν+1​.

The exact result with D=4−2ϵD=4-2\epsilonD=4−2ϵ is

Iν=Γ(ν−D/2)Γ(ν)1(m2)ν−D/2 . I_\nu = \frac {\Gamma(\nu-D/2)} {\Gamma(\nu)} \frac 1 {(m^2)^{\nu-D/2}} \, . Iν​=Γ(ν)Γ(ν−D/2)​(m2)ν−D/21​.

The IBP identity agrees with the exact result using the gamma function relations Γ(ν+1)=νΓ(ν)\Gamma(\nu+1) = \nu \Gamma(\nu)Γ(ν+1)=νΓ(ν), Γ(ν+1−D/2)=(ν−D/2)Γ(ν−D/2)\Gamma(\nu+1-D/2) = (\nu-D/2) \Gamma(\nu-D/2)Γ(ν+1−D/2)=(ν−D/2)Γ(ν−D/2).

Basic setup for bubble example

Let's look at the bubble family of integrals,

Iν1,ν2=∫dDkiπD/21(−k2+m2)ν1[−(p−k)2+m2]ν2=∫dDkiπD/21ρ1ν1 ρ2ν2 ,\begin{aligned} I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} &= \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \frac{1} {(-k^2+m^2)^{\nu_1} [-(p-k)^2+m^2]^{\nu_2}} \\ &= \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \frac 1 {\rho_1^{\nu_1} \, \rho_2^{\nu_2}} \, , \end{aligned}Iν1​,ν2​​​=∫iπD/2dDk​(−k2+m2)ν1​[−(p−k)2+m2]ν2​1​=∫iπD/2dDk​ρ1ν1​​ρ2ν2​​1​,​

where we defined

ρ1=−k2+m2,ρ2=−(p−k)2+m2 . \rho_1 = -k^2 + m^2, \quad \rho_2 = -(p-k)^2+m^2 \, . ρ1​=−k2+m2,ρ2​=−(p−k)2+m2.

We can invert the above equation to write any dot product involving the loop momentum kkk in terms of inverse propagator variables,

k2=−ρ1+m2,p⋅k=12(ρ2−ρ1+p2) . k^2 = -\rho_1 + m^2, \quad p \cdot k = \frac 1 2 (\rho_2 - \rho_1 + p^2) \, . k2=−ρ1​+m2,p⋅k=21​(ρ2​−ρ1​+p2).

We can write down the IBP identity

0=∫dDkiπD/2∂∂kμkμρ1ν1ρ2ν2=∫dDkiπD/2[Dρ1ν1ρ2ν2−ν1ρ1ν1+1ρ2ν2 kμ∂∂kμρ1−ν2ρ1ν1ρ2ν2+1 kμ∂∂kμρ2]\begin{aligned} 0 &= \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial k^\mu} {\frac{k^\mu}{\rho_1^{\nu_1} \rho_2^{\nu_2}}} \\ &= \int \frac {d^D k}{i \pi^{D/2}} \left[ \frac D {\rho_1^{\nu_1} \rho_2^{\nu_2}} - \frac {\nu_1} {\rho_1^{\nu_1+1} \rho_2^{\nu_2}} \, k^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial k^\mu} \rho_1 - \frac {\nu_2} {\rho_1^{\nu_1} \rho_2^{\nu_2+1}} \, k^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial k^\mu} \rho_2 \right] \end{aligned}0​=∫iπD/2dDk​∂kμ∂​ρ1ν1​​ρ2ν2​​kμ​=∫iπD/2dDk​[ρ1ν1​​ρ2ν2​​D​−ρ1ν1​+1​ρ2ν2​​ν1​​kμ∂kμ∂​ρ1​−ρ1ν1​​ρ2ν2​+1​ν2​​kμ∂kμ∂​ρ2​]​

Using Eqs. (8) and (9), we have

kμ∂∂kμρ1=−2k2=2ρ1−2m2kμ∂∂kμρ2=2p⋅k−2k2=ρ1+ρ2−2m2+p2 .\begin{aligned} k^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial k^\mu} \rho_1 &= -2 k^2 = 2 \rho_1 - 2m^2 \\ k^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial k^\mu} \rho_2 &= 2 p \cdot k - 2 k^2 = \rho_1 + \rho_2 - 2m^2 + p^2 \, . \end{aligned}kμ∂kμ∂​ρ1​kμ∂kμ∂​ρ2​​=−2k2=2ρ1​−2m2=2p⋅k−2k2=ρ1​+ρ2​−2m2+p2.​

Systematic reduction of all possible bubble integrals

Here we present a systematic method for solving IBP relations bubble integral. The method will be applicable to many other Feynman integrals. We will reduce bubble integrals Iν1,ν2I_{\nu_1, \nu_2}Iν1​,ν2​​ with any integer indices ν1,ν2\nu_1, \nu_2ν1​,ν2​ without relying on symmetry relations. All we need is one more IBP identity besides Eq. (10), by changing kμk^\mukμ in the numerator to pμp^\mupμ. (For a general integral family, we allow the numerator to be any of the independent external and loop momenta.) The calculation steps are exactly analogous to those leading to Eq. (12), and we just quote the final result which is the counterpart of Eq. (12),

0=(ν2−ν1)Iν1,ν2+ν1Iν1+1,ν2−1−ν2Iν1−1,ν2+1+ν1p2Iν1+1,ν2−ν2p2Iν1,ν2+1 . 0 = (\nu_2-\nu_1) I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} + \nu_1 I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2-1} - \nu_2 I_{\nu_1-1, \nu_2+1} + \nu_1 p^2 I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2} - \nu_2 p^2 I_{\nu_1, \nu_2+1} \, . 0=(ν2​−ν1​)Iν1​,ν2​​+ν1​Iν1​+1,ν2​−1​−ν2​Iν1​−1,ν2​+1​+ν1​p2Iν1​+1,ν2​​−ν2​p2Iν1​,ν2​+1​.

Eq. (12) and Eq. (17) involve a total of 5 integrals, for every choice of integers (ν1,ν2)(\nu_1, \nu_2)(ν1​,ν2​). The integrals involved in the two linear relations are visualized on the (ν1,ν2)(\nu_1, \nu_2)(ν1​,ν2​) lattice,

For the sake of solving the system, we define an auxiliary quantity, the complexity of an integral Iν1,ν2I_{\nu_1, \nu_2}Iν1​,ν2​​, to be ν1+ν2\nu_1+\nu_2ν1​+ν2​. We want to solve more "complex" integrals in terms of less complex ones. Two of the integrals have the same complexity. Fortunately, we have two IBP identities Eq. (12) and (17) which allow us to solve both integrals in terms of the three less complex integrals - the 2×22\times 22×2 linear system is non-degenerate if ν1≥1,ν2≥1\nu_1 \geq 1, \nu_2 \geq 1ν1​≥1,ν2​≥1. The results are called reduction rules,

Iν1+1,ν2=1p2(4m2−p2)[−(2m2−p2)Iν1+1,ν2−1+2ν2ν1m2Iν1−1,ν2+1−Dp2−ν1(2m2+p2)+2ν2(m2−p2)ν1Iν1,ν2] ,Iν1,ν2+1=1p2(4m2−p2)[−(2m2−p2)Iν1−1,ν2+1+2ν1ν2m2Iν1+1,ν2−1−Dp2−ν2(2m2+p2)+2ν1(m2−p2)ν2Iν1,ν2] .\begin{aligned} I_{\nu_1 + 1, \nu_2} = \frac{1}{p^2 (4m^2-p^2)} & \bigg[ -(2m^2-p^2) I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2-1} + 2\frac{\nu_2}{\nu_1} m^2 I_{\nu_1-1, \nu_2+1} \\ & \quad - \frac {Dp^2 - \nu_1(2m^2+p^2) + 2\nu_2(m^2-p^2)}{\nu_1} I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} \bigg] \, , \\ I_{\nu_1, \nu_2+1} = \frac{1}{p^2 (4m^2-p^2)} & \bigg[ -(2m^2-p^2) I_{\nu_1-1, \nu_2+1} + 2\frac{\nu_1}{\nu_2} m^2 I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2-1} \\ & \quad - \frac {Dp^2 - \nu_2(2m^2+p^2) + 2\nu_1(m^2-p^2)}{\nu_2} I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} \bigg] \, . \end{aligned}Iν1​+1,ν2​​=p2(4m2−p2)1​Iν1​,ν2​+1​=p2(4m2−p2)1​​[−(2m2−p2)Iν1​+1,ν2​−1​+2ν1​ν2​​m2Iν1​−1,ν2​+1​−ν1​Dp2−ν1​(2m2+p2)+2ν2​(m2−p2)​Iν1​,ν2​​],[−(2m2−p2)Iν1​−1,ν2​+1​+2ν2​ν1​​m2Iν1​+1,ν2​−1​−ν2​Dp2−ν2​(2m2+p2)+2ν1​(m2−p2)​Iν1​,ν2​​].​

Furthermore, the tadpole IBP identity Eq. (5) can be reused,

(D−2ν1)Iν1,0+2m2ν1Iν1+1,0=0,(D−2ν2)I0,ν2+2m2ν2I0,ν2+1=0 . (D-2\nu_1) I_{\nu_1,0} + 2m^2 \nu_1 I_{\nu_1+1,0} = 0 , \quad (D-2\nu_2) I_{0, \nu_2} + 2m^2 \nu_2 I_{0, \nu_2+1} = 0 \, . (D−2ν1​)Iν1​,0​+2m2ν1​Iν1​+1,0​=0,(D−2ν2​)I0,ν2​​+2m2ν2​I0,ν2​+1​=0.

Advanced topics:

  • For more complicated integrals, deriving reduction rules is very nontrivial and often prohibitive. Instead, a systematic method known as the Laporta algorithm is used to automate IBP reduction. Some public codes include e.g. LiteRed, FIRE, Kira. Future lectures will rely on results obtained from such software without further derivation.

  • The number of master integrals is finite for any integral family. There is some geometric understanding, e.g. in terms of the Euler characteristics of the singular surface of the U+FU+FU+F polynomial in the Lee-Pomeransky representation, or in terms of critical points of maximal-cut integrals in the Baikov representation. The mathematical language of twisted cohomology formalizes the concepts of IBP reduction and the number of master integrals.
