Toy example: IBP for tadpole integrals

Let's look at the simplest toy example, the family of one-loop tadpole integrals with internal mass mmm,

Iν=∫ddqiπd/21(q2−m2)ν=∫ddqiπd/21ρν ,\begin{aligned} I_{\nu} &= \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \frac{1} {(q^2-m^2)^{\nu}} \\ &= \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \frac 1 {\rho^{\nu}} \, , \end{aligned}Iν​​=∫iπd/2ddq​(q2−m2)ν1​=∫iπd/2ddq​ρν1​,​

where we defined

ρ=q2−m2 . \rho = q^2 - m^2 \, . ρ=q2−m2.

The IBP identity is

0=∫ddqiπd/2∂∂qμqμρν=∫ddqiπd/2[dρν−νρν+1qμ∂∂qμρ] .\begin{aligned} 0 &= \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial q^\mu} \frac{q^\mu}{\rho^{\nu} } &= \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \left[ \frac {d} {\rho^\nu} - \frac {\nu} {\rho^{\nu+1}} q^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial q^\mu} \rho \right] \, . \end{aligned}0​=∫iπd/2ddq​∂qμ∂​ρνqμ​​=∫iπd/2ddq​[ρνd​−ρν+1ν​qμ∂qμ∂​ρ].​

We use

qμ∂∂qμρ=2q2=2(ρ+m2) . q^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial q^\mu} \rho = 2q^2 = 2 (\rho + m^2) \, . qμ∂qμ∂​ρ=2q2=2(ρ+m2).

The IBP identity becomes

0=∫ddqiπd/2[d−2νρν−2m2νρν+1]=(d−2ν)Iν−2m2νIν+1 . 0 = \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \left[ \frac {d - 2 \nu} {\rho^\nu} - \frac{2 m^2 \nu}{\rho^{\nu+1}} \right] = (d-2\nu) I_\nu - 2m^2 \nu I_{\nu+1} \, . 0=∫iπd/2ddq​[ρνd−2ν​−ρν+12m2ν​]=(d−2ν)Iν​−2m2νIν+1​.

The exact result with d=4−2ϵd=4-2\epsilond=4−2ϵ is

Iν=(−1)νΓ(ν−d/2)Γ(ν)1(m2)ν−d/2 . I_\nu = (-1)^\nu \frac {\Gamma(\nu-d/2)} {\Gamma(\nu)} \frac 1 {(m^2)^{\nu-d/2}} \, . Iν​=(−1)νΓ(ν)Γ(ν−d/2)​(m2)ν−d/21​.

The IBP identity agrees with the exact result using the gamma function relations Γ(ν+1)=νΓ(ν)\Gamma(\nu+1) = \nu \Gamma(\nu)Γ(ν+1)=νΓ(ν), Γ(ν+1−d/2)=(ν−d/2)Γ(ν−d/2)\Gamma(\nu+1-d/2) = (\nu-d/2) \Gamma(\nu-d/2)Γ(ν+1−d/2)=(ν−d/2)Γ(ν−d/2).

Basic setup for bubble example

Let's look at the bubble family of integrals,

Iν1,ν2=∫ddqiπd/21(q2−m2)ν1[(p+q)2−m2]ν2=∫ddqiπd/21ρ1ν1 ρ2ν2 ,\begin{aligned} I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} &= \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \frac{1} {(q^2-m^2)^{\nu_1} [(p+q)^2-m^2]^{\nu_2}} \\ &= \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \frac 1 {\rho_1^{\nu_1} \, \rho_2^{\nu_2}} \, , \end{aligned}Iν1​,ν2​​​=∫iπd/2ddq​(q2−m2)ν1​[(p+q)2−m2]ν2​1​=∫iπd/2ddq​ρ1ν1​​ρ2ν2​​1​,​

where we defined

ρ1=q2−m2,ρ2=(p+q)2−m2 . \rho_1 = q^2 - m^2, \quad \rho_2 = (p+q)^2-m^2 \, . ρ1​=q2−m2,ρ2​=(p+q)2−m2.

We can invert the above equation to write any dot product involving the loop momentum qqq in terms of inverse propagator variables,

q2=ρ1+m2,p⋅q=12(ρ2−ρ1−p2) . q^2 = \rho_1 + m^2, \quad p \cdot q = \frac 1 2 (\rho_2 - \rho_1 - p^2) \, . q2=ρ1​+m2,p⋅q=21​(ρ2​−ρ1​−p2).

We can write down the IBP identity

0=∫ddqiπd/2∂∂qμqμρ1ν1ρ2ν2=∫ddqiπd/2[dρ1ν1ρ2ν2−ν1ρ1ν1+1ρ2ν2 qμ∂∂qμρ1−ν2ρ1ν1ρ2ν2+1 qμ∂∂qμρ2]\begin{aligned} 0 &= \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \frac{\partial}{\partial q^\mu} {\frac{q^\mu}{\rho_1^{\nu_1} \rho_2^{\nu_2}}} \\ &= \int \frac {d^d q}{i \pi^{d/2}} \left[ \frac d {\rho_1^{\nu_1} \rho_2^{\nu_2}} - \frac {\nu_1} {\rho_1^{\nu_1+1} \rho_2^{\nu_2}} \, q^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial q^\mu} \rho_1 - \frac {\nu_2} {\rho_1^{\nu_1} \rho_2^{\nu_2+1}} \, q^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial q^\mu} \rho_2 \right] \end{aligned}0​=∫iπd/2ddq​∂qμ∂​ρ1ν1​​ρ2ν2​​qμ​=∫iπd/2ddq​[ρ1ν1​​ρ2ν2​​d​−ρ1ν1​+1​ρ2ν2​​ν1​​qμ∂qμ∂​ρ1​−ρ1ν1​​ρ2ν2​+1​ν2​​qμ∂qμ∂​ρ2​]​

Using Eqs. (8) and (9), we have

qμ∂∂qμρ1=2q2=2ρ1+2m2qμ∂∂qμρ2=2p⋅q+2q2=ρ1+ρ2+2m2−p2 .\begin{aligned} q^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial q^\mu} \rho_1 &= 2 q^2 = 2 \rho_1 + 2m^2 \\ q^\mu \frac{\partial}{\partial q^\mu} \rho_2 &= 2 p \cdot q + 2 q^2 = \rho_1 + \rho_2 + 2m^2 - p^2 \, . \end{aligned}qμ∂qμ∂​ρ1​qμ∂qμ∂​ρ2​​=2q2=2ρ1​+2m2=2p⋅q+2q2=ρ1​+ρ2​+2m2−p2.​

The IBP identity in the 2nd line of Eq. (4) evaluates to

0=(d−2ν1−ν2)Iν1,ν2−2ν1m2Iν1+1,ν2−ν2Iν1−1,ν2+1−ν2(2m2−p2)Iν1,ν2+1\begin{aligned} 0 = (d-2\nu_1-\nu_2) I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} - 2\nu_1 m^2 I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2} - \nu_2 I_{\nu_1-1, \nu_2+1} - \nu_2 (2m^2-p^2) I_{\nu_1, \nu_2+1} \end{aligned}0=(d−2ν1​−ν2​)Iν1​,ν2​​−2ν1​m2Iν1​+1,ν2​​−ν2​Iν1​−1,ν2​+1​−ν2​(2m2−p2)Iν1​,ν2​+1​​

Systematic reduction of all possible bubble integrals

Here we present a systematic method for solving IBP relations bubble integral. The method will be applicable to many other Feynman integrals. We will reduce bubble integrals Iν1,ν2I_{\nu_1, \nu_2}Iν1​,ν2​​ with any integer indices ν1,ν2\nu_1, \nu_2ν1​,ν2​ without relying on symmetry relations. All we need is one more IBP identity besides Eq. (10), by changing qμq^\muqμ in the numerator to pμp^\mupμ. (For a general integral family, we allow the numerator to be any of the independent external and loop momenta.) The calculation steps are exactly analogous to those leading to Eq. (12), and we just quote the final result which is the counterpart of Eq. (12),

0=(ν1−ν2)Iν1,ν2−ν1Iν1+1,ν2−1+ν2Iν1−1,ν2+1+ν1p2Iν1+1,ν2−ν2p2Iν1,ν2+1 . 0 = (\nu_1-\nu_2) I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} - \nu_1 I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2-1} + \nu_2 I_{\nu_1-1, \nu_2+1} + \nu_1 p^2 I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2} - \nu_2 p^2 I_{\nu_1, \nu_2+1} \, . 0=(ν1​−ν2​)Iν1​,ν2​​−ν1​Iν1​+1,ν2​−1​+ν2​Iν1​−1,ν2​+1​+ν1​p2Iν1​+1,ν2​​−ν2​p2Iν1​,ν2​+1​.

Eq. (12) and Eq. (17) involve a total of 5 integrals, for every choice of integers (ν1,ν2)(\nu_1, \nu_2)(ν1​,ν2​). The integrals involved in the two linear relations are visualized on the (ν1,ν2)(\nu_1, \nu_2)(ν1​,ν2​) lattice,

For the sake of solving the system, we define an auxiliary quantity, the complexity of an integral Iν1,ν2I_{\nu_1, \nu_2}Iν1​,ν2​​, to be ν1+ν2\nu_1+\nu_2ν1​+ν2​. We want to solve more "complex" integrals in terms of less complex ones. Two of the integrals have the same complexity. Fortunately, we have two IBP identities Eq. (12) and (17) which allow us to solve both integrals in terms of the three less complex integrals - the 2×22\times 22×2 linear system is non-degenerate if ν1≥1,ν2≥1\nu_1 \geq 1, \nu_2 \geq 1ν1​≥1,ν2​≥1. The results are

Iν1+1,ν2=1p2(4m2−p2)[(2m2−p2)Iν1+1,ν2−1−2ν2ν1m2Iν1−1,ν2+1+dp2−ν1(2m2+p2)+2ν2(m2−p2)ν1Iν1,ν2] ,Iν1,ν2+1=1p2(4m2−p2)[(2m2−p2)Iν1−1,ν2+1−2ν1ν2m2Iν1+1,ν2−1+dp2−ν2(2m2+p2)+2ν1(m2−p2)ν2Iν1,ν2] .\begin{aligned} I_{\nu_1 + 1, \nu_2} = \frac{1}{p^2 (4m^2-p^2)} & \bigg[ (2m^2-p^2) I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2-1} - 2\frac{\nu_2}{\nu_1} m^2 I_{\nu_1-1, \nu_2+1} \\ & \quad + \frac {dp^2 - \nu_1(2m^2+p^2) + 2\nu_2(m^2-p^2)}{\nu_1} I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} \bigg] \, , \\ I_{\nu_1, \nu_2+1} = \frac{1}{p^2 (4m^2-p^2)} & \bigg[ (2m^2-p^2) I_{\nu_1-1, \nu_2+1} - 2\frac{\nu_1}{\nu_2} m^2 I_{\nu_1+1, \nu_2-1} \\ & \quad + \frac {dp^2 - \nu_2(2m^2+p^2) + 2\nu_1(m^2-p^2)}{\nu_2} I_{\nu_1, \nu_2} \bigg] \, . \end{aligned}Iν1​+1,ν2​​=p2(4m2−p2)1​Iν1​,ν2​+1​=p2(4m2−p2)1​​[(2m2−p2)Iν1​+1,ν2​−1​−2ν1​ν2​​m2Iν1​−1,ν2​+1​+ν1​dp2−ν1​(2m2+p2)+2ν2​(m2−p2)​Iν1​,ν2​​],[(2m2−p2)Iν1​−1,ν2​+1​−2ν2​ν1​​m2Iν1​+1,ν2​−1​+ν2​dp2−ν2​(2m2+p2)+2ν1​(m2−p2)​Iν1​,ν2​​].​

Applying these reduction formulas iteratively, we reduce the complexity of the integrals until all integral are reduced to I1,1I_{1,1}I1,1​, I2,0I_{2,0}I2,0​ and I0,2I_{0,2}I0,2​.

Using FIRE software to automate IBP reduction

You need to have Wolfram Mathematica installed.

On Linux or Mac, install FIRE 6.5 with the following command in the terminal:

git clone https://gitlab.com/feynmanIntegrals/fire.git

We'll skip the installation of the C++ version of FIRE, since we'll only use the Mathematica version here.

Navigate into the examples/ folder:

cd fire/FIRE6/examples

and open the example notebook box.nb. You only need to look at the first half of the notebook, leading to the reduction result for F[1, {2, 2, 2, 2}], i.e. the box integral with all four propagators raised to a 2nd power.
